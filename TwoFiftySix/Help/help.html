Powers of Two is an implementation of 2048 by Gabriele Cirulli, created in 2014 and still available on the App Store. My goal was to write a simplified version, eliminating the “extras” that have accumulated over the years: no ads, no score, no undo, no sound effects, no power-ups, and completely free and open source.

How to Play

You are shown a 4x4 game board, with slots for 16 tiles. A slot either contains one tile or is empty. Every tile displays a numeric value, which is a power of 2: it can be 2, or 4, or 8, or 16, and so on. The game begins by randomly producing two tiles, whose values are each probably 2 but possibly 4.

You make a move by swiping in one of the four cardinal directions: up, down, left, or right. You don’t have to swipe on the game board; you can swipe anywhere on the screen.

The game board responds to your move in the following way:

  1. All the tiles on the board, within their respective row or column, slide as far as possible in the direction that you swiped.
  2. If two tiles are adjacent along the axis of your swipe, and if they have the same values as one another, they are merged into a single tile, whose value is the sum of the values of the original two tiles.
  3. The game then randomly produces one new tile, whose value is probably 2 but possibly 4. The move ends, and now you can make another move.

The more times a given tile gets another tile merged into it, the higher its value becomes. Successive merges on a single tile will produce a tile whose value is the next power of 2: two adjacent 2s become a single 4, two adjacent 4s become a single 8, two adjacent 8s become a single 16, and so on.

The object of the game is to get one tile, the highest-numbered tile on the board, to be as high as possible:

  • 512 is considered very good.
  • 1024 is considered excellent.
  • 2048 is considered the best; if you reach it, you have effectively won the game (hence the game’s original name, “2048”).

The game ends when you tap the New Game button.

  • This might be because you have reached the 2048 tile. Congratulations, you have won! If you do reach 2048, you are welcome to play on, but it is extremely unlikely that you would reach a higher tile value, so you should probably just pat yourself on the back and tap the New Game button.
  • Alternatively, this might be because you cannot make another move. This would be because all 16 slots are filled with tiles, and there are no adjacent same-valued tiles in any swipe direction, so that no merges are possible. At that point, you are stuck; your only option is to tap the New Game button.

Fine Points

In this section, I’ll just clarify in detail the mechanics of how the game works.

  • During a move, the force of gravity and the power of merging operate simultaneously along the axis of your swipe. For example: a row or column (along the axis of your swipe) might contain 2, empty, 2, empty; in that case, the two 2s are brought together and merged all in one moment, resulting in 4, empty, empty, empty.
  • If a single row or column consists of two adjacent same-valued pairs (along the axis of your swipe), both pairs are merged (and the two merged tiles end up next to each other). For example: 2, 2, 4, 4 becomes 4, 8, empty, empty.
  • If a single row or column contains three same-valued tiles (along the axis of your swipe), so that they all end up adjacent, the two tiles furthest in the direction of your swipe are merged. For example: 2, 2, 2, empty becomes 4, 2, empty, empty.
  • The same tile cannot undergo two merges during a single move. For example: 2, 2, 2, 2 (along the axis of your swipe) will result in 4, 4, empty, empty; the two 4s resulting from merging and made adjacent by your move are not merged into an 8 on the same move. Of course, you can then swipe again along the same axis if you wish to merge the new pair.
  • The hand-waving phrase describing how the game produces a new tile whose value is “probably 2 but possibly 4” has a well-defined meaning: A new tile is placed completely at random into any of the slots that are empty after your move, and its value is randomly 2 or 4, where on average 2 appears nine times out of ten.
  • If you swipe in a certain direction but no move in that direction is possible — because all tiles are already positioned as far as possible in that direction, and there are no adjacent same-valued tiles to merge along that axis — then your swipe gesture is considered null and void. There is no move, and no new tile is produced.

How to Win

You are in a race against the game board’s inexorable production of new tiles. The board is trying to fill up with tiles; you are trying to create empty slots, which you do by performing merges.

Empty slots represent degrees of freedom. The more empty slots there are, the more random will be the placement of each new tile, and so the more likely it is that when the board produces a new tile, it will be good for you — a useful value in a useful place — and not bad for you.

Conversely, imagine letting the board become so crowded with tiles that at the end of your move there is just one empty slot. In that case, there are basically no degrees of freedom at all: the new tile will with 100% certainty appear in that slot, and the only thing in doubt is whether it will be a 2 or a 4. And at that point, if no merge is possible in any direction, the game is over; the board is full and you are stuck.

At the same time, you are also trying to make high-valued tiles — in particular, one high-valued tile that represents the game’s result. Your goal is to merge pairs of high-valued tiles to make an even higher-valued tile. You want to maintain enough high-valued tiles to be useful to that end.

On the other hand, you do not want to maintain so many high-valued tiles as to occupy too much space. High-valued tiles reduce the number of empty slots — which, as already stated, represent degrees of freedom. Moreover, high-valued tiles are not useful in the short term, because the board is going to produce only 2s and 4s, which cannot immediately be merged with any higher-valued tile.

In addition, it becomes harder to merge a tile as its value gets higher — because it is harder to make another tile of the same value. Again, the heart of the problem is that the board is going to produce only 2s and 4s. From there it is easy to make an 8, and since two 8s are pretty easy to make, it is pretty easy to merge them into a 16. But a 64, say, is noticeably more difficult to merge, because to do so, you need two of them, and they need to be adjacent, and your raw material is just 2s and 4s, which must be combined from the bottom up, as it were, to obtain another 64.

It would be a lot easier, though, to make that second 64 if you happened already to have a nearby 32 lying around — because it is easier to combine 2s and 4s to make another 32 than to combine 2s and 4s to make a 64 from scratch. And it would be a lot easier to make that second 32 if you happened already to have a nearby 16 lying around, for the same reason. And so on.

The preceding observation points the way to an optimal balance between your conflicting desires: You should try to accumulate your high-valued tiles in the form of a chain, where each adjacent tile is the previous power of 2. That way, your high-valued tiles are occupying space, which is bad, but if you can just form a new tile matching the lowest value at one end of the chain, you will be able to merge successively all the way up the chain and double the highest value at the other end of the chain. This is called collapsing the chain.

For example, here’s an actual position from a recent game of mine:

In the above screenshot, I’ve reached a high tile of 512. Here are some things to notice:

  1. The bottom row is a chain of accumulated high-value tiles. Contrast this with all the other tiles, which are evidently the detritus of the board’s inexorable tendency to create 2s.
  2. The chain fills the bottom row. This is not coincidence. I formed the chain this way on purpose, because it means that I can make the maximum number of types of move without disturbing the chain: I can swipe left or right without disturbing the chain, because the chain fills the bottom row (and no tile in the chain can be merged with its sideways neighbor), and I can swipe down without disturbing the chain, because the chain fills the bottom row. In fact, none of the tiles in the bottom row will move, unless I am compelled by bad luck or stupidity to swipe up; that would likely be a disaster.
  3. The 512, the high tile, is in the corner. This means that the chain can be a full four tiles long — it fills the bottom row — and thus is so long that the tile at the low end of the chain, the 64, is vastly easier to match and merge than the tile at the high end. The longer the chain, the easier it will be make a new low-end tile that matches it, and so collapse the chain and double the high tile.

This notion of collapsing the chain lies at the heart of the chain’s purpose. Clearly, in the position shown in the preceding screenshot, if I can somehow make another 64 tile appear where the second 4 tile is now, I can make a series of merges: swipe down to merge the two 64s into a 128 (where the 64 is now), then swipe left to merge the two 128s into a 256 (where the 128 is now), then swipe left to merge the two 256s into 512 (where the 256 is now), then swipe left to merge the two 512s into a 1024.

(Obviously, while I’m spending four moves collapsing the chain, the board will be generating four new tiles. But that’s not bad; it’s good! Those four new tiles cannot possibly interfere with the chain, because the chain’s tiles are immobile and adjacent. And the new tiles will all be 2s and 4s, which will help me get started working my way up to form new high tiles.)

Well, making a 64 tile is not easy, but it’s not terribly difficult either. One approach is simply to continue the chain. That way, I don’t actually need to make a second 64; I just need two 32s, which are even easier to make. And I don’t actually need a second 32; I just need two 16s, which are even easier to make! And so on.

When I’ve done that, all the tiles in the chain are gone, and the 512 tile has turned into a 1024 tile:

Having reached 1024, you might proceed to attempt to reach 2048. This is time-consuming, and can prove more difficult than reaching 1024.

To reach 2048, you basically have to rebuild from scratch the entire chain culminating in 512 that you just destroyed by collapsing it to reach 1024 in the first place. This is a longer chain and therefore occupies more room than the previous chain, so you have that much less room in which to operate. Meanwhile at every moment things threaten to go wrong, and the stakes are that much higher when they do.

On the other hand, even when things do go wrong and the board becomes somewhat disorderly, it still might be possible to rescue the situation long enough to reach 2048. My attitude is that I don’t really care how I reach 2048, because I won’t be trying to go any further.

The point is that 2048 is achievable; if you’re diligent, you should, with practice, patience, and experience, be able to get there in better than a quarter of your games — perhaps as much as half your games. But you shouldn’t expect to reach 2048 on every game, because there’s a major element of luck involved. At any moment, a tile produced by the board in a bad place can make the best-laid plans go badly awry. Sometimes you can compensate; sometimes you can’t. Have fun!
